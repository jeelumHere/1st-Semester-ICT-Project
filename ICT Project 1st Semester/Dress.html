Traditional Dresses of Pakistan

Explore the Tradition and Fashion of Pakistan



National Attire

The national dress of Pakistan is the shalwar kameez, worn by both men and women across the country. It reflects the cultural identity, traditions, and heritage of the Pakistani people. The dress is comfortable, elegant, and suitable for all seasons. Men usually wear it with a waistcoat, while women style it with dupattas and beautiful embroidery. It represents unity in diversity, as every province adds its own unique designs and colors to this traditional outfit.

National Women Wear

The national dress for women in Pakistan is the shalwar kameez, often paired with a dupatta. Women wear it in many styles, with different fabrics, colors, and traditional embroidery. Each region adds its own unique patterns, making the outfit rich in diversity. The shalwar kameez beautifully represents the grace and cultural identity of Pakistani women.

National Men Wear

The national dress for Pakistani men is the shalwar kameez.It is comfortable, traditional, and worn in all provinces.Men often pair it with a waistcoat for a more formal look.The outfit reflects cultural pride and simplicity.

Punjabi Attire

Traditional Punjabi attire is characterized by vibrant colors and the iconic Salwar Kameez, often styled as a pleated Patiala suit for women. This is usually paired with a stunning Phulkari dupatta, while men wear a Kurta Pajama topped with a symbolic Turban. The ensemble is traditionally finished with handcrafted leather Juttis worn by both genders. These outfits perfectly capture the spirited and energetic culture of the region.

Punjabi Women Wear

Traditional Punjabi women's attire centers on the vibrant Salwar Kameez, a comfortable and versatile three-piece ensemble. The most celebrated style is the Patiala suit, characterized by its elegantly pleated, voluminous lower garment paired with a short kurti. This outfit is traditionally completed with a colorful Phulkari dupatta, a shawl featuring intricate, hand-stitched floral embroidery that symbolizes prosperity. These garments, often finished with handcrafted Juttis, perfectly embody the spirited and feminine grace of Punjabi culture.

Punjabi Men Wear

Punjabi men traditionally wear the comfortable Kurta Pajama, with the vibrant and symbolic Turban (Pagri) as their defining accessory. For festive or rural settings, the Kurta is often paired with a wrap-around cloth called a Tehmat. The entire look is completed with handcrafted leather Juttis, reflecting a style that is both practical and dignified.

Sindhi Attire

Traditional Sindhi women wear the long Cholo or Julaba tunic over wide trousers (Suthan). Their attire is defined by extensive mirror work (Abla) and vibrant needlework, often using light fabrics suitable for the climate. They use the Ajrak shawl or a Ralli quilt as a wrap, complementing the look with heavy silver jewelry. These unique outfits powerfully showcase the region's rich, artistic culture.

Sindhi Women Wear

Traditional Sindhi women wear the long tunic known as the Cholo or Julaba over wide trousers (Suthan). Their garments are famously recognized by the dazzling, intricate mirror work (Abla) and vibrant needlework that covers the fabric. They often use the distinctive Ajrak shawl or a colorful Ralli quilt as a wrap, showcasing the region's rich artistic heritage. They often adorn themselves with heavy, traditional silver jewelry, including large necklaces and elaborate bangles, to complete their ceremonial looks.

Sindhi Men Wear

Traditional Sindhi men primarily wear a Kurta (tunic) over wide trousers called Sindhi Shalwar or Suthan. Their ensemble is defined by two key accessories: the distinctive block-printed Ajrak shawl draped over the shoulders and the round, embroidered Sindhi Cap (Topi). These comfortable and symbolic garments reflect the region's strong cultural identity and rich heritage. The Ajrak itself is highly versatile and is used ceremonially, often tied as a turban or presented as a token of honor. The attire is traditionally finished with simple, comfortable leather slip-on shoes or sandals.

Balochi Attire

Balochi attire is known for its modesty and cultural pride. Women wear a loose dress (pashk) heavily adorned with intricate, colorful hand-embroidery (doch), often including a large pocket, which can indicate tribal origin. Men wear a loose shalwar kameez with a voluminous turban (pag), providing practical comfort and a strong visual identity.

Balochi Women Wear

The traditional attire for Balochi women is the loose, ankle-length pashk, defined by its exquisite, vibrant hand-embroidery (doch) across the bodice and a signature large pocket. This intricate needlework is a vital cultural element, proudly signifying the woman's tribal affiliation and heritage.

Balochi Men Wear

The traditional attire for Balochi men is the voluminous shalwar kameez, chosen for its comfort and excellent ventilation in the hot climate. This outfit is universally paired with the highly recognizable, large, expertly-wrapped turban (pag or dastaar), which is essential for sun protection and serves as a key symbol of Baloch dignity and cultural identity.

Pakhtoon Attire

Pakhtoon (Pashtun) attire is rooted in the loose-fitting shalwar kameez, prioritizing modesty, dignity, and practicality for the mountainous climate. For men, the outfit is the Perahan wa Tunban (tunic and baggy trousers), often paired with a waistcoat (waskat) for layering. The essential cultural marker is the distinctive headwear, such as the large turban (patkay or lungi) or the rolled woolen cap (Pakol). Women traditionally wear the firaq partug (a long, full-skirted dress over trousers) which is famously decorated with vibrant beads and complex embroidery across the bodice and shoulders, particularly for special occasions. Ultimately, all traditional Pashtun clothing utilizes loose cuts and durable fabrics to facilitate movement and protect the wearer in the harsh terrain.

Pakhtoon Women Wear

The traditional Pakhtoon (Pashtun) women's attire is the firaq partug, a three-piece ensemble of a long, full-skirted dress (firaq) over loose trousers (partug), completed with a headscarf (chador). Crucially, the firaq is characterized by its vibrant, heavy embroidery and beading—especially on the shoulders—which showcases the wearer's craftsmanship and cultural identity. For formal wear, rich fabrics like silk or velvet in deep colors are used, making the outfit a beautiful statement of Pashtun heritage.

Pakhtoon Men Wear

The traditional dress for Pakhtoon men is the Perahan wa Tunban, consisting of an extra-long, loose tunic and extremely baggy trousers, essential for comfort and movement in the rough terrain. This foundational outfit is almost always complemented by a waistcoat and the highly significant headwear: either a large, expertly-wrapped turban (Patkay or Lungi) or the soft,woolen Pakol cap. These elements combine to create a look that embodies practicality,modesty,and deep cultural honor.

Gilgiti Attire

Gilgiti attire is defined by its adaptation to the cold, featuring heavy woolen robes (Chugha) over a shalwar kameez. The essential men's accessory is the soft, rolled-rim Gilgiti cap (Khoi), often adorned with a feather for dignity. Women wear loose dresses and a distinctive, embroidered cap (Iraghi) with traditional silver jewelry.

Gilgiti Women Wear

Gilgiti women wear a loose, long tunic (pashan) over shalwar, often crafted from durable, colorful fabrics. The most recognizable component is the distinctive embroidered cap (Iraghi or Nating). Formal dresses feature rich embroidery and beadwork on the bodice and sleeves. The look is finalized with traditional silver jewelry adorning the cap and clothing, reflecting their unique mountain culture.

Gilgiti Men Wear

The traditional Gilgiti men's dress is founded upon the shalwar kameez worn beneath a heavy, long woolen cloak known as the Chugha or Shuqa, essential for protection from the harsh cold. The defining cultural marker is the soft, white woolen Gilgiti cap (Khoi or Phartsun) with a rolled rim. This cap is often adorned with a feather or plume for formal occasions, symbolizing the wearer's honor and dignity. The attire as a whole is a highly practical and proud expression of their high-altitude heritage.

Kashmiri Attire

Traditional Kashmiri dress centers on the Pheran (or Phiran), a long, loose, gown-like robe worn by both men and women, designed primarily to provide warmth against the cold climate. Women's Pherans are highly decorative, showcasing exquisite embroidery like Tilla (metallic thread) or Sozni (fine needlepoint) work. Men's Pherans are simpler, focused on utility. Both styles are often paired with a Pashmina shawl and distinctive traditional headwear, making the attire a practical yet elegant expression of Kashmiri culture.

Kashmiri Women Wear

Kashmiri women traditionally wear the Pheran (Phiran), a long, loose robe made distinctive by rich, intricate embroidery (Tilla or Sozni work) on the neckline and cuffs. This practical, warm garment is combined with unique traditional headwear, like the Kasaba (padded cap) or the Taranga (trailing headscarf). The attire is often finalized with a luxurious Pashmina shawl, creating a highly elegant and culturally expressive look.

Kashmiri Men Wear

Kashmiri men's traditional attire is the Pheran (Phiran), a long, loose, gown-like robe typically made of thick wool or tweed for protection during the harsh winter. Unlike the women's version, the men's Pheran is generally plain and unembellished, emphasizing utility, warmth, and simplicity. This garment allows for the carrying of a Kangri (fire pot) underneath for personal heating. The outfit is often completed with a waistcoat and traditional headwear, such as the Karakul fur cap or a simple skullcap.
